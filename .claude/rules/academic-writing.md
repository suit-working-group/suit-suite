# Academic Writing Standards

## Evidence-Based Argumentation
- Every factual claim MUST be supported by at least one verifiable source
- Target citation density: minimum 1 citation per argumentative paragraph
- Never make quantitative claims without a source (percentages, statistics, performance data)
- When citing standards or regulations, always include the specific clause/article number

## Citation Accuracy — MANDATORY
- NEVER cite a reference that does not directly support the **specific claim** being made. A paper on the right topic but the wrong mechanism is a bad citation.
- Before using a citation, verify that the paper's abstract/findings actually demonstrate the claim. Do not assume support based on title or topic proximity.
- If no reference in the bibliography supports a claim, either reformulate the claim as a stated principle (without pretending empirical backing) or find a genuinely supporting reference.
- Self-test for every `\cite{}`: "Does this paper's abstract specifically demonstrate *this exact assertion*?" If not, remove the citation or reformulate the claim.

## Concession-Then-Rebuttal Pattern
- When addressing counter-arguments, first acknowledge partial merit honestly
- Use the pattern: "This concern has merit in [specific scope]... However, [evidence shows]..."
- Never strawman an objection — reformulate it at its strongest before responding
- Name logical fallacies explicitly when identified (ad hominem, status quo bias, genetic fallacy, false equivalence)

## Evidence Stratification
- Build arguments through cumulative, independent layers:
  1. Binding legal instruments (legislation, case law)
  2. Normative frameworks and standards (NIST, ISO, COBIT)
  3. Sector consensus (professional bodies, surveys)
  4. Empirical data (quantified measurements, incident reports)
  5. Institutional precedents (named organizations with verifiable practices)
- Each thesis should be supported by at least 3 independent layers
- If one layer is contested, the others must sustain the argument independently

## Intellectual Transparency
- Disclose methodology and limitations explicitly
- Acknowledge risks honestly (use risk registers with probability/impact)
- Disclose use of AI tools in document composition
- Distinguish clearly between established facts, professional consensus, and author's interpretation
- Use burden-of-proof language when appropriate: specify what evidence would be needed to refute a claim

## Anti-Vulgarization — MANDATORY
- NEVER present a statistic or finding in isolation if it encourages the reader to retain an oversimplified or misleading takeaway.
- Always present quantitative claims with their qualifying context: study scope, language, model, prompting strategy, sample size, developer experience level.
- When presenting limitations or risks, ALWAYS pair them with observed improvements or mitigations — let the reader form a balanced understanding.
- Prefer references showing nuanced, representative findings over dramatic single-metric claims.
- Self-test for every quantitative claim: "Would a reader quoting just this sentence get an accurate picture?" If not, add qualifying context or restructure comparatively.

## Balanced Technology Framing — MANDATORY (especially for AI/LLM topics)
- LLMs do nothing without being directed by prompts and context. NEVER write text implying LLMs autonomously produce good or bad outcomes.
- Always distinguish between **what LLMs produce** (output quality given a specific prompt/context) and **what developers do with LLMs** (workflow, review discipline, process structure).
- The differentiating factor is almost always the human-directed workflow, not the model's intrinsic capability. Frame findings accordingly.
- When LLMs are demonstrably good or bad at specific tasks, state it — but only when empirically demonstrated, and with the study's conditions.
- Structure AI quality/productivity discussions as: (1) what improves with structured use, (2) what degrades with unstructured use, (3) the human process as the differentiating factor.
- Use paired evidence: e.g., one reference showing improvement with structured process + one reference showing degradation without structure.

## Rhetorical Discipline
- Avoid inflammatory or emotional language — let evidence carry the argument
- Use precise qualifiers: "demonstrates" (strong evidence), "suggests" (moderate), "may indicate" (weak)
- Italicize foreign phrases and technical Latin (ad hominem, status quo, carte blanche)
- When quoting authority figures, always provide full attribution with date
